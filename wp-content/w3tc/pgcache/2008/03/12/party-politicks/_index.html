Party Politicks

I noticed REDvolution party members at a CLASS Senate candidate debate I attended frequently shouting to vote a “straight ticket” or to “vote REDvolution!” It’s curious that this mentality is coupled with SGA’s recent decision to remove party labels from the ballots.

SGA members and those apologetic to the SGA’s legislation and will have howled at the foul reporting produced by the Daily Cougar. That “strictly” speaking “parties won’t appear on the ballot” is their defense. It’s not a “rule,” they argue further, that CLASS Senate hopefuls and SGA constituents cannot assemble and band together (otherwise, unconstitutionality could be charged); it is only that the ballot has undergone some structural readjustment, a layout change. It seems something like that the SGA came to a conclusion: a legislative decision for a typeface change was necessary. The ballot got a fancy new dress.

It’s been argued that the point of the ballot redesign obtained an immediate and agreeable effect. Now those manic devils wishing to fight for halting the increase in student frees, albeit its imminence, or to put recycle bins where they’re obviously due must rumble and sweat along the campaign trail for their seats. That’s likely; or is it?

During the Question-Answer round, questions such as “Why haven’t you guys been [grassroots] campaigning?” and “How do you expect us to trust that you’ll have time to ‘fight’ for us if you don’t campaign?” were raised. Oh, they campaigned: through Facebook, with posters, that sort. A majority of the CLASS Senate candidates, except for a few (I recall one being REDvolution.), threw themselves at the mercy of the audience. Obstacles such as “work” and “school” seemed to furnish a sweeping credence to a false dilemma. Our devils are looking less frightening, less politically astute.

Most interesting was the mention of M.D. Anderson Library and its possible Starbucks project. “How would you respond if a Starbucks were to appear in the library before the library were to become a 24/7 gig for, as has been presumptuously put, late-night toiling away at the books?” So how would you?

Asked before that were a few lightning rounders: Do you support 100% Fair Trade coffee on campus? Do you think the library should never close? Many said “no,” and all said “yes.” So I said interesting: the relationship between the Fair Trade movement on campus and that sweet library spot is an intimate yet seemingly forgotten one.

I doubt any candidate at that debate has read bill UB44002, passed by the Senate of the 44th SGA on April 4th, 2007, that reads “The location of the 100% Fair Trade Certified coffee kiosk will be in the 24 hour lounge in the M.D. Anderson Library, and should be established within a reasonable time frame.”

Quickly now: Do those who answered “no” not support SGA’s informed legislative decision-making? Do they, without pause, reject giving Starbucks competitors a chance, even a small kiosk of a chance? What is the point of even asking such a bloated and feeble question? If you mind the bill, the question of “Is a 100% Fair Trade campus pretty cool?” is worthless and ineffectual. The bill has already been passed. Read it, and get on it, candidates.

The redress of the ballot presumably forced Senate candidates to campaign, averting their chances of riding on party success and affiliation, but, as was admitted, campaigning is discourse for the backburner. “You just can’t expect us to fit that in too!” Perhaps the SGA shot its own foot with its legislative decision—or at least potential SGA members’ feet.

But shouldn’t the removal of party labels from the ballot carry a more idealistic and commendable tune? I doubt “Vote straight REDvolution,” say, properly soothes anyone’s ears. Regardless of what the “rules” say, students are criticizing political character, and the SGA should expect that “removing the label” or similar desktop publishing gimmickry does not solve the actual problem of party politicking.
