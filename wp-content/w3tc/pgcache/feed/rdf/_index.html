 Finally done with the idea, I s’pose. The design concept is based in a problem of personal organization style: Given that one’s writing style will emerge and disrupt a design over time, how does one maintain connection between types of content? — My answer is a command-line-esque interface which listens for hypertext entries, littered over the page and strategically/thematically named. It’ll need polish, but nevertheless: This is A weedy florilegium (try me out).

Technical details

For type: I’m using a mix of Pigeon from losttype, an Austin-based type co-op suggested by a dear friend, and Philosopher from the Google Web Fonts library. The layout, constructed on 960.gs. Yes, yes, yes, wordpress. Repo here. –

I do not consider it novel by any stretch. I sat on the design of the toolbar long before finding the updated (12/2011) google.com toolbar hack. Of course, it’s pretty self-evident that commandline-based terminals aren’t new, only forgotten. I’ve only recently initiated myself in this world of mouse-less computing. And I believe it a skill only capable of exactness through experience, but at the cost of losing various modes of thinking which find source in habit and conclusion. I cannot be any less vague. But loss of linguistic form and grace is what it takes.

For color the color palette… What the #fck7f4? I don’t see color.

I’ve tested in maybe two browsers? Then again, I test on just about every device within smelling distance. Internet Explorer isn’t a browser. It’s an internet explorer. Its chief purpose is to enable unfettered bidirectional communication between you and the world. Every attempt at exploration has fundamentally changed the course of human-kind. How noble. The internet is a vast array of — I can’t do this… Get a real browser.

And if you don’t like scrolling, go read a newspaper.

The b and i tags are still valid and have a measure of applicability. Please read the specification before you attempt to instruct others.

It shows a misunderstanding of the phrase “more semantic” when one says that “strong elements are more semantic than b elements.” Not only is this not the case, but it makes no sense for it to be the case. The applicability of b and applicability of strong are mutually exclusive. For one, it is obvious that strong has a connotation to represent on top of the text itself where as b has no connotation beyond stylistic marking. Style is the only property they share, while their uses determine their meaning. We don’t mean to only suggest, and nothing more, “this is italic text” or “this is bold text.” If the task were that simply, sure, we would have redundancy in the markup, and so on. But “more semantic” suggests a measure of applicability. So surely strict observance of the specification which describes the criteria for use, and key awareness of doctype, is markedly instrumental to discussion. Worth a shot, no?

Any mention of the linguistic markings which go on in academic literature, European, American, etc will easily attest to the major role of the b and i elements’ and their meaning. Think of i as having an implicit meta-data taxonomization which may describe dialectic or historical data; we think this when identifying the Latin mid-sentence. In a web design context, think of b as the most basic textual representation of a “call to action.” b represents a kind of thingness on the page. One way to look at them is with fear, as a developer, due to the verb implication of b’s. They don’t have much to say linguistically, in terms of the content itself or otherwise, but they do shoot upwards the idea that the text itself, as a whole message, stands in a certain relation to the web page. b, then, seems to me, under the worst case scenario, like a UI hook.

Think of b as the “prompt” for inline elements.

 Well here’s a find.

Sanford Alexander Sr. was born a slave in South Carolina in 1823. His exact arrival in Sandtown Community in Neshoba County near the Bogue Chitto Community is not known. He was purchased at a slave market in New Orleans, Louisiana, by the Alexander family and brought to this area. Sanford was freed in 1865 in Neshoba County, Mississippi, at the age of 42. He lived 64 years as a free man in Neshoba and Kemper Counties. Sanford was the father of 10 children from two marriages, this expanded the Alexander name for over six generations.

Sanford was a successful farmer who acquired several hundred acres of land. The offspring of this former slave are productive citizens who are proud of their heritage. His grandchildren and great-great-grandchildren are successful lawyers, doctors, corporate managers, computer programmers, pilots, professional football players, cattle ranchers, insurance agents, underwriters, educators and entrepreneurs. He died in 1929 at the age of 106.

Sanford was buried in the Liberty United Methodist Church Cemetery in Collinsville, Mississippi. His grandson, the late Kutcher Threefoot Alexander, Sr., purchased a memorial headstone for his grave. Kutcher Threefoot Alexander found and preserved a portrait of our great-grandfather.

Sanford was married to Charlotte and Mandy. His children are: Mary Alexander Polluck, Mike Alexander, Frank Alexander, Daughty Alexander McClain, Miles Alexander, Lewis Alexander (father of Kutcher), Jerry Alexander, John Alexander, Maranda Alexander Donald and Mary Louise “Lou” Alexander Hall.

From “Kemper County, Mississippi – A Pictorial History”, placed here with permission by the Kemper County Historical Commission.
